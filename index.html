about Rizal

Jose Protacio Rizal Mercado y Alonzo Realonda

Jose Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines, was born in 1861 in Calamba, Laguna, to an educated and affluent family. His early education, guided by his mother, instilled in him strong moral values. Despite facing discrimination, he excelled academically and later studied at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila and the University of Santo Tomas. Rizal's involvement in various fields such as poetry, drama, and athletics showcased his diverse talents and interests. His experiences of injustice fueled his desire for social reform, leading him to pursue further studies in Europe. In Madrid and Paris, he became involved in nationalist movements and wrote influential novels exposing Spanish colonial injustices. Through his writings, particularly "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," he advocated for reforms and called for an end to oppressive practices.

Despite facing threats and persecution from the Spanish authorities, Rizal remained steadfast in his commitment to the Filipino people and their aspirations for freedom. His martyrdom at the hands of the colonial regime further fueled the flames of resistance and inspired generations of Filipinos to continue the struggle for independence. Rizal's legacy extends beyond his role as a nationalist hero; he is also remembered for his contributions to education, healthcare, and social welfare in the Philippines. His life and teachings continue to inspire Filipinos to uphold the values of integrity, patriotism, and solidarity in the pursuit of a just and prosperous society.

Education

Rizal developed a strong foundation in literacy and moral values under the guidance of his mother, Teodora Alonso. This early education instilled in him a love for learning and a sense of responsibility towards his community.

Rizal attended a local school in Biñan, Laguna, where he studied under the supervision of Justiniano Aquino Cruz. He excelled in his studies despite facing discrimination due to his mixed heritage.

Achievement

Despite facing discrimination, Rizal excelled in his studies at the local school in Biñan, demonstrating his intellectual capabilities and determination to overcome obstacles.

At the age of 11, Rizal entered the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, a prestigious Jesuit-run institution. He completed his Bachelor of Arts degree, where he demonstrated excellence in academics and extracurricular activities.

Achievement

Rizal achieved academic excellence at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, where he received numerous awards and honors for his academic performance and extracurricular achievements. He also honed his skills in various fields, including poetry, drama, and athletics.

University of Santo Tomas (1877-1882)

Rizal pursued a degree in Medicine at the University of Santo Tomas in Manila, one of the oldest universities in Asia. However, his dissatisfaction with the prevailing racial discrimination and hostility from Dominican friars led him to seek further education abroad.

Achievement

While at the University of Santo Tomas, Rizal continued to excel academically, but he also encountered resistance and discrimination from Dominican friars. Despite these challenges, he completed his coursework and gained valuable insights into the injustices of colonial society.

Travels of Jose Rizal

Europe (1882-1887)

Rizal pursued higher studies in Madrid, Spain, where he completed a degree in medicine and took courses in philosophy and letters. He also traveled to other European cities, including Paris, Heidelberg, and Berlin, to further his education and engage with various intellectual circles.

Hong Kong (1888)

Rizal temporarily lived in Hong Kong to escape political persecution and to work on his novel "Noli Me Tangere," which addressed the social issues and injustices in the Philippines.

Germany (1886-1887)

While in Europe, Rizal spent a considerable amount of time in Germany, particularly in Heidelberg and Wilhelmsfeld. It was during this period that he wrote several significant works, including the annotation of Antonio Morga's "Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas."

Spain (1890)

Rizal returned to Spain to publish his novels and advocate for reforms in the Philippines. He faced challenges and opposition from both Spanish authorities and Filipino detractors.

Philippines (1892-1896)

Rizal returned to the Philippines and established the La Liga Filipina, a civic organization aimed at uniting Filipinos for reforms. However, his activities drew the attention of Spanish authorities, leading to his exile to Dapitan, Mindanao.

Dapitan (1892-1896)

During his exile in Dapitan, Rizal continued his intellectual pursuits, engaging in various activities such as farming, teaching, and even contributing to the improvement of the community.

Cuba and Spain (1896)

In an effort to join the revolution against Spanish rule, Rizal sought permission to serve as a doctor in the Cuban war. However, Spanish authorities arrested him in Barcelona and brought him back to the Philippines to face charges of rebellion, sedition, and conspiracy.

Manila (1896)

Rizal faced a trial and was convicted of the charges against him. He was executed by firing squad on December 30, 1896, at Bagumbayan, now Rizal Park in Manila.

Life Works of Rizal

Noli Me Tangere & El Filibusterismo

"Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo" are important because they awakened nationalist sentiments, exposed colonial oppression, advocated for reform, inspired revolution, and stand as literary masterpieces in Philippine literature.

"Noli Me Tangere," published in 1887, stands as a cornerstone of Philippine literature and history. With its title translating to "Touch Me Not" in Latin, the novel serves as a powerful social commentary on the injustices inflicted by Spanish colonial authorities and the clergy upon the Filipino people during the late 19th century. Through vivid characters and compelling storytelling, Rizal skillfully depicts the harsh realities of colonial rule, showcasing the pervasive corruption, oppression, and hypocrisy prevalent in Philippine society. The protagonist, Crisostomo Ibarra, returns to the Philippines from Europe only to confront the systemic injustices and abuses perpetrated by the ruling class. Rizal's primary objective with "Noli Me Tangere" was to shed light on these injustices, galvanizing Filipinos to seek reform and fight for their rights. Today, the novel remains an essential component of Filipino education, revered as one of the most significant literary works in Philippine history, inspiring generations to stand against oppression and strive for justice and equality.

"El Filibusterismo," published in 1891 as the sequel to "Noli Me Tangere," continues José Rizal's exploration of t he Philippine society under Spanish colonial rule. Its title, translating to "The Reign of Greed" or "The Filibuster," reflects the novel's themes of corruption and oppression. Building upon its predecessor's foundation, "El Filibusterismo" delves deeper into the injustices faced by the Filipino people and the simmering discontent that leads to rebellion. The protagonist, Simoun, emerges as a new identity for Crisostomo Ibarra from "Noli Me Tangere," with a calculated plan to incite revolution against the Spanish authorities. Through Simoun's character, Rizal intricately examines the complexities of resistance and revolution, highlighting the moral dilemmas and consequences associated with pursuing change through violent means. "El Filibusterismo" not only solidifies Rizal's reputation as a nationalist writer and thinker but also serves as a critical reflection on the shortcomings of the Philippine Revolution. Like its predecessor, this novel is celebrated as a classic of Philippine literature, studied extensively in educational institutions for its profound insights into colonial oppression and the quest for liberation.
